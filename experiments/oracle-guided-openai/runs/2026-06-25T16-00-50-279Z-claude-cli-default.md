### Control

Here's the honest core of it: the question you're actually asking isn't "will the startup succeed?" — nobody can answer that, including the founders. The answerable question is **"if it fails, what happens to me, and can I live with that?"** Most people fixate on the first question because it's dramatic, and underweight the second, which is the one they can actually reason about.

So let me push on the things that genuinely move this decision, because the answer is different depending on them:

**The downside, concretely.** You're "good at" your stable job. That's a bigger deal than it sounds — it means the failure case probably isn't "ruin," it's "spend a few months finding another stable job, possibly the same kind." If your skills are in demand and you have, say, 6+ months of runway, then the thing that feels like a cliff is actually a step down you can climb back up. The risk you're feeling is mostly *emotional volatility*, not *catastrophic loss*. Those deserve very different weights. But if you have dependents, meaningful debt, a visa tied to employment, or a thin savings cushion — then the downside is real and you should de-risk before leaping (negotiate a later start, build runway, etc.), not just feel your way into it.

**What specifically thrills you — and is it durable.** "Thrills me" splits into reasons that survive contact with reality and reasons that don't. The work itself, the problem, the people you'd build with, the rate you'd learn — those are durable; they pay off even if the company dies. Infatuation with the *idea* of a startup, the equity lottery ticket, or escape from your current boredom — those fade in about six months. Be ruthless with yourself about which bucket you're in. A good test: imagine the company folds in 18 months. Do you still think "I'm glad I did that"? If yes, the thrill is the durable kind.

**Diagnose the boredom.** "No longer excited" — is that this specific job, or this *type* of work, or just a plateau? Because if it's a plateau, the startup will thrill you for a while and then you'll plateau there too. The startup is worth leaving for if it's pointing you at work you actually want to be doing, not just at novelty. Novelty is not a strategy.

**Regret asymmetry.** Picture yourself in ten years. The regret of "I tried it and it failed" is almost always smaller and cleaner than "I had the offer in front of me, it lit me up, and I talked myself out of it." Failed attempts become stories and skills and networks. The un-taken leap becomes a low hum of *what if* that doesn't go away. If you're early-to-mid career, this asymmetry tilts hard toward going.

**My recommendation:** if the downside is survivable — you can find work again and you won't be financially wrecked for a year or two — **go.** The emotional data you're giving me ("thrills me," "no longer excited") is not noise; it's information about where your energy actually is, and you spend most of your waking life at work. Stable-but-flat has a hidden cost people discount: it quietly compounds into a decade. The startup might fail, but you almost certainly won't.

I'd **reverse that** only if one of these is true: you have real dependents/obligations and no cushion; the "thrill" is mostly escape and equity rather than the work itself; or the startup's specifics are alarming (no runway, no salary, a founder you don't trust, a product with no path to revenue). "Tiny and might fail" is normal. "Can't make payroll in three months and the founders are cagey about it" is a different thing — so do the unsexy diligence: ask how many months of cash they have, what the last raise was, what the plan is if the next one doesn't land.

What's your actual downside if it folds in a year — and when you imagine that exact outcome, are you glad you went or relieved you didn't?

### Control

Reach out. I'll make the case, because I think the framing in your question is doing some quiet work against you.

**"Reopening it" is smuggling in a false equivalence.** You're treating "reach out" and "reopen the disagreement" as the same act. They're not. You can send a warm, low-stakes message that doesn't touch the argument at all — "Been thinking about you, miss you, hope you're well." That's not reopening anything. The fear that contact = conflict is exactly what keeps these silences going, on both sides.

**"Let it rest" is the more dangerous option, not the safe one.** Resting implies the thing is fine and just needs quiet. But a close friendship that's gone silent after a disagreement isn't resting — it's calcifying. Every week of silence adds a new layer: now it's not just the disagreement, it's the silence *about* the disagreement, and then the awkwardness of having let it go this long. Time doesn't neutralize these things; it makes the eventual reach-out heavier. The cheapest moment to call is always now.

**Consider the asymmetry of regret.** If you reach out and it's clumsy or they're cool about it, you lose a little pride and an afternoon of feeling exposed. If you don't, you risk losing a close friend by default — not through any decision, just through nobody being willing to go first. Those are not symmetric stakes. One is recoverable in a day; the other you might still be thinking about in five years.

A few specifics that matter:

- **Lead with warmth, not the autopsy.** Don't open with "about what happened…" Open with the friendship. If the disagreement needs addressing, let them choose when — or offer a single clean line ("I think I was short with you and I've regretted it") and then drop it. A brief, genuine ownership of your part disarms almost everything. Over-explaining re-litigates.
- **Don't make them guess your intent.** Silence is ambiguous; they may read your quiet as anger the same way you're reading theirs. Naming that you value them removes the ambiguity that's probably feeding both sides.
- **Reach out without a scoreboard.** Go in willing for them to be slow or guarded at first. If you need them to immediately match your warmth to call it worth it, you'll read a normal hesitation as rejection.

The one case where I'd say wait: if the "small disagreement" was actually you noticing something that needs to change — they crossed a real line, or the friendship was costing you something — then silence might be information, not a problem to fix. But you called it *small*, and you're clearly still drawn to the person. That's not a relationship you want to lose to inertia.

Send the message. Keep it short and warm. Go first.
